Introduction

Throughout the lifecycle of a software project, one of the golden rules of project management entails maintaining a consistent level of engagement between the project development team and the project/product’s relevant stakeholders. Stakeholders are the life force of virtually all professional projects, and the success of these projects is ultimately contingent on stakeholder contribution and communication. Through imprudently making decisions that negatively impact one of the myriad groups of stakeholders involved in your project, this could directly influence these stakeholders’ perception of you and your team, even leading to incrimination. Of course, it is certainly likely that you do not intentionally harm these highly valued groups; on the contrary, this could probably be attributed to ineffectively identifying the stakeholders who both influence and are affected by your projects. Through properly identifying stakeholders, you can initiate a strong relationship that will maximize their potential to ensure your projects’ success.

Identifying Stakeholders

From an initial, cursory glance at your project requirements and planning documents, identifying stakeholders may seem like a relatively simple task; however, to this day, project managers are institutionally redefining what does and does not constitute a project stakeholder. For example, stakeholders can be either internal or external to a project. Internal stakeholders often refer to the project team, sponsors and managers directly involved in the development organization. External stakeholders not only include your demographic and projected user base, but also competitors who may capitalize on missed opportunities, or even suppliers and corporate partners who would reap benefits from your projects. With this virtually innumerable number of stakeholders considered, it is evident that a methodical, tried-and-true approach would be quite helpful for identifying relevant groups of stakeholders.

Assessing the Candidates

As you are determining who may and may not qualify as potential project stakeholders, it is essential to document the potential candidates and measure their relationship to the project at hand. One way to accomplish this is through a stakeholder register that identifies each stakeholder or group of stakeholders, assesses the relationships between them and the project, and classifies them as either internal or external stakeholders. However, including every individual who has even a modicum of influence on a project can be wasteful of both time and resources. Something that can help create a more modest, yet still highly effective, list is to ask yourself a series of questions as you inspect your stakeholder register. These questions include:

  • Does the stakeholder fundamentally impact your organization’s ability to develop a successful product?
  • Can you clearly identify what you want from the stakeholder?
  • Do you want to improve the relationship with this stakeholder?
  • Is the stakeholder dispensable or easily substituted by another individual or group?
  • Does another relationship satisfy everything this stakeholder brings to the table?

Of course, this is only a small set of questions that you should consider when determining which ones should remain in the stakeholder register, but trimming the fat can help you focus on what is most important and carries the greatest degree of influence.

Stakeholder Analysis

Once you have created a slimmer stakeholder register, one of the most effective techniques to follow is what is known as a stakeholder analysis. This delves into determining how you should effectively manage stakeholder relationships by classifying stakeholders using a variety of relevant stakeholder classes. A power/interest grid is a useful tool that you can leverage to prioritize stakeholders and measure what level of care should be taken to maintain a positive relationship with each respective class. A power/interest grid simplifies things by introducing only four categories in which each stakeholder falls: high power, high interest; high power, low interest; low power, high interest; lastly, low power, low interest. Of course, those with both high power and high interest are the upper echelon of stakeholders and should be assigned the greatest priority; however, even the low power, low interest stakeholders should be acknowledged and given some level of respect throughout a project’s development. However, this is only one of the many tools that can be used to help you analyze your stakeholder groups.

Wrap-Up

In conclusion, it is evident that identifying stakeholders can be a much more difficult task than it would initially seem; fortunately, through utilizing an effective methodology to analyze potential stakeholders, you can assuredly harbor a greater degree of insurance for your project and be able to engage the most essential groups throughout your project’s planning and development. Through developing and refining a stakeholder register, you can form a relatively concise list that explicitly reveals each stakeholder group along with the influence that they would have on a particular project. Furthermore, a variety of helpful tools and techniques, such as power/interest grids, exist that can help facilitate the identification and prioritization process. Ultimately, through proficiently identifying the important individuals and groups that hold a stake in your project, you will be able to more effectively ascertain project requirements and goals to ensure a successful product.
