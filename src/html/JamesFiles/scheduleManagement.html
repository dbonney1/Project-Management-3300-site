Project Schedule Management

Another of the major knowledge areas of project development includes project schedule management. This aspect refers to the timing of the project, which involves task fulfillment estimates, timely completion, and overall planning/scheduling. In this article, we will explore parts of this knowledge area in a question based format, for ease of studying and memory retention. For each question, an explanation will be provided discussing the reasoning behind each correct answer.

Question 1: Which of the following is NOT an aspect of project schedule management?

A. In project schedule management, the next step after sequencing activities is to develop the schedule.

B. An advantage of the project management software is that it has the capacity to calculate the critical path(s) for a project.

C. A dependency pertains to the sequencing of project activities or tasks.

D. Knowing the amount of float allows project managers to know whether a project schedule is flexible.

...The answer to this question is A! All of the statements included are true statements besides this one. In project schedule management, the sequence of steps is…

  • 1. Plan Schedule Management
  • 2. Define Activities
  • 3. Sequence Activities
  • 4. Estimate Activity Durations
  • 5. Develop Schedule

and as you can see, the next step is actually to estimate activity durations. Not doing so before developing the schedule could result in a completely irrelevant schedule that falls apart upon execution of the project schedule management process. Imagine if you came up with the schedule assuming that coding the backend would take 1 week, but it actually required 2 months! That would be a costly mistake for the entire organization.

Question 2: Which of the following is NOT an aspect of project schedule management?

A. ‘Free slack’ is used for the amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying the early start date of any immediately following activities

B. The critical path on a project can change as the project progresses

C. Feeding buffers protect tasks on the critical chain from being delayed.

D. In an AOA network diagram, a ‘merge’ occurs when two or more activities follow a single node.

...The answer to this question is D! There is a slight inaccuracy here. The term ‘merge’ here should be replaced with ‘burst.’ It’s important to understand that a burst is as stated, when two activities follow a single node on the chart. A merge is actually when two or more nodes precede a single node on the network diagram chart. It’s important to understand these concepts personally and deeply in order to communicate them in the future in a professional environment. Gaining a common understanding with your colleagues on the mechanisms of these communicative tools will definitely increase your productivity in the long run.

Question 3: Which of the following is NOT an aspect of project schedule management?

A. Parkinson’s law states that work expands to fill the time allowed.

B. A schedule management plan includes information which describes the format and frequency of schedule reports required for the project.

C. Finish to finish is a dependency in which the “from” activity must be finished before the “to” activity can be finished.

D. The arrows in a network diagram represent missed milestones in a project.

...The answer to this question is D! Network diagrams are useful tools for displaying activity sequencing. They are composed of nodes and arrows. The arrows in a network diagram actually represent activities or even relationships between tasks, while nodes are the start and end points of activities. Arrows do NOT represent missed milestones in the project. While it is important to keep track of missed milestones, that’s not the focus of this specific concept. As stated before, gaining a true understanding of network diagrams enables you to communicate on a higher level of abstraction with your team members, so it’s important to understand these concepts.

Another topic to mention would be scope creep, which is a critically important concept in this knowledge area, referring to the tendency for project scope to keep getting bigger and bigger, or “creep”. Oftentimes, it can be tempting to raise the requirements and focus of the project as the developers and designers themselves become increasingly ambitious about what they can accomplish. This leads to the over-expansion of requirements and expectations, which are doomed to disappointingly fall short. One famous example can be found in the ubiquitous McDonald’s Restaurants. In 2001, McDonald’s Restaurants launched an ambitious initiative to implement a large interconnected intranet network which would allow for real time monitoring of many extremely detailed metrics and metadata. Its scope would include every single store(many tens of thousands) in 120 countries. McDonald’s invested $170 million on the idea, only for it to be prematurely terminated(McDougall). Ambition is often an extremely necessary part of any initiative, but left unchecked, it can ruin any progress made in the first place.
